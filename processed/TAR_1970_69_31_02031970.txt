EDITORIAL
Dirty utensils. Food displayed in open cases ready to be 
fondled by undecided hands. Bugs in the salads, Dirt on the 
celery. Employees hair in the beef stew. These and many 
other observations force us to disagree with the judgment 
made Saturday that the sanitary conditions in Skibo food ser
vice are excellent.
We understand and recognize that running a food ser
vice of this magnitude involves many dangers and that it is 
difficult, perhaps impossible, to police every phase of the 
operation. Yet these examples of negligence cannot be ex
cused and must be corrected.
It is disturbing that the report of the investigating com
mittees indicates that, (a) the illness was a freak occurence, 
(b) there is virtually nothing that can be done to improve 
sanitary conditions in Skibo food service, and (c) since bac
teria cannot be detected at an early stage, it cannot be con
trolled. Although the report stated that a review of current 
procedures is going to be conducted, the logical conclusion 
from statement number one that conditions are excellent, 
to statement number two that a review is underway, leads us 
to believe that the purpose of the review is to reassure the 
campus community. We do not need reassurance; we do 
need improvement.
The plague which infected the campus last week must 
be considered as a warning to the possibilities of much more 
serious illnesses which can occur from mishandled food. We 
cannot brush it off as a freak accident. No matter how timeconsuming or costly, an investigation must be made to dis
cover whether the staphloccus was present in the packaged 
food when delivered or whether the bacteria originated with
in the confines of our own operation.
Food Service this year has certainly improved. Under the 
directorship of Mr. Prillaman the food service operation is 
breaking even for the first time in many years, and the ser
vice and selection has improved in both the snack bar and 
dining rooms. It would be unfair to ignore these changes.
At the same time, to ignore the fact that in many areas 
food service can be improved would be negligent. Employees 
must begin to look cleaner, utensils should not have traces of 
their former contents, the kitchen garbage must be disposed 
rather than kept in corners, bugs must be directed elsewhere, 
and the food must be properly cooked. Carelessness in the 
handling of food could be disastrous.
What would happen, if due to negligence pork were 
served undercooked? Trichonosis is not a joke. It is a possi
bility?
