EDITORIAL
p oatteh Ul MUsRehs
Last Thursday night we discovered a little about the 
values of a sizable sector of the campus population. Maybe 
life is held cheap on campus because nobody has to pay 
for it until he leaves. Everybody lives in a cell, concerned 
with what happens inside, only distracted by external 
events when they perturb the status quo. Students are wor
ried about grades, jobs, the draft, or a weekend date, 
"vital matters within the bounds of their own realm. Vir
tually nobody has taken the effort to look over his wall at 
whats happening in the real world.
By now everybody knows of the death of Dr. Martin 
Luther King and the waves of violence which followed. 
We could not miss seeing the reaction of the majority of 
the campus community to this tragedy. On receiving the 
word that Friday classes were cancelled they swung right 
into the weekend without breaking stride, staging im
promptu parties Thursday night and Friday. We were ap
palled at those we saw running around cheering the days 
occurrence. There was a water battle, great for an idle 
spring afternoon, but shockingly out of context then. The 
beautiful people held first-magnitude beer blasts, since 
they could sleep late Friday without consequence and had 
no reason to sweat waking up hung over. It was the soft, 
sweet life.
There were some, when the first flush of holiday wore 
off, who considered the repercussions of last weeks events, 
but how many bothered to realize what the causes were, 
or cared enough to do something that might prevent recur
rence of what has happened this spring or last summer, or 
what has been happening for the past decade. Its easier 
to turn away and ignore the real world, or to hope to get 
a rifle and play Sergeant Fury and the Howling Comman
dos. Except this is for real. The neat wall that used to keep 
out the outside world wont hold any longer. Its past time 
to start thinking beyond yourselves; otherwise there may 
not be very much of the real world left to consider.
