DIARY OF A COED
MONDAY: Prof. Pomfritt sprang quiz in English lit this 
morning. If Shakespeare didnt write Canterbury Tale# Im a 
dead duck . . . Lunch at the houseturkey hash. Question: 
how can we have turkey hash when we never had turkey7*. . . 
Smoked a Marlboro after lunch. I dig those better makins 
the most! . . . Played bridge with sorors in afternoon. When 
game was over, my partner stabbed me several times with 
hatpin. Must learn weak club bid . . . Dinner at houselamb 
hash. Question: how can we have lamb hash when we never had 
Iamb? . . . Smoked a Marlboro after dinner. What filter! What 
flavor! What pack or box!... Chapter meeting at night. Motion 
made to abolish capital punishment for pledges. Motion de
feated ... Smoked more Marl boros. Quelle joie!... And so to bed.
TUESDAY: Faculty tea at the house. Spilled pot of oolong 
on Dean of Women. She very surly. Offered her a Marlboro. 
Still surly. Offered skin graft. No help . . . Dinner at Kozy 
Kampus Kafe24 hamburgers. But no dessert. Have to watch 
waistline . . . And so to bed.
WEDNESDAY: Got our marks in English lit quiz. Lucky 
for me Shakespeare wrote Canterbury Tales! ... Afternoon date 
with Ralph Feldspar. Purely platonic. Ralph wanted to con
sult me about love trouble hes having with his girl Nymphet 
Calloway. I assured him things would get better. Ralph said 
lie certainly hopes so because last four times he called on 
Nymphet, slie dumped vacuum cleaner bag on him ... Smoked 
several Marlboros. Wonderful cigarette. No confusion about 
which end to light. Saves loads of time . . . Dinner at house 
bread. Thats all; just bread . . . And so to bed.
THURSDAY: Three packages from homelaundry, cookies, 
records. So hungry I ate all three ... Quiz in American history. 
If Millard Fillmore didnt invent cotton gin, Im in big trouble 
..  Dinner at house. Big excitementNymphet Calloway an
nounced her engagement to Ralph Feldspar. While sorors 
flocked around to congratulate Nymphet, I ate everybodys 
side meat... Then smoked Marlboro. Oh, what a piece of work 
is Marlboro!... And so to bed.
FRIDAY; Got our marks in American history quiz. Was 
shattered to learn that Millard Fillmore did not invent cotton 
gin. He wrote Canterbury Tales ... How very odd!... Lunch 
at the housebread hash... Marlboro after lunch. Great smoke. 
Must send valentine to manufacturers . . . Spent entire after
noon getting dressed for date tonight with Norman Twonkey. 
Norman is dall, dark, loadeda perfect doll! Only thing wrong 
is he never tells a girl where hes going to take her. So I put 
on a bathing suit, on top of that an evening gown, and on top

Teenianity
By David Stock
It has often been said that a civilization can best be judg
ed by the level of its popular arts and entertainment. What, 
then, will the historians of the future think when they ex
amine the amusements of our day? In the field of music, the 
public taste is at an all time low. One is afraid to turn on 
the radio because of the horrible noises which the disc jockeys 
spew forth. The television set is constantly defiled by a 
parade of quiz shows, soap operas, western, and similar inan
ities. The most active sport in which most people indulge is 
keeping-up-with-the-Joneses. Reading is used for amusement 
in the world of 1959 approximately as much as falconry. The 
average Hollywood movie is beyond description.
The cause of much of this nausea is the sudden rise of 
a new religion in America, which might be called Teenianity, 
that is, the worship of the teenager. Somehow, someone de
cided that the adolescent years, which had formerly been 
considered merely a period of transition between childhood 
and adulthood, were really the finest time of ones life, and 
therefore should be prolonged as much as possible. America 
probably has more midde-aged adolescents than any other 
country in the world. Rock and roll is the best evidence imag
inable for this view. Fabian, Rickey Nelson, and the like 
are lionized as heroes of American culture by the teenagers 
and juvenile adults who support them, while Russia salutes 
Van Cliburn.
Disc Jockeys
Disc-jockeys control, through the teenagers, a billiondollar industry, where lack of talent is a sure sign of suc
cess. They play recordings which the most primitive culture 
on this planet would reject as being childish and unmusical.
Next turn to television. Not since the days of the Roman 
gladitorial bouts has there been anything to compare with 
TV wrestling. Can one imagine anything more sadistic than 
quiz shows, honest or otherwise, where every second of the 
contestants agony is projected into the living rooms of thirty 
million people? King Midas greed was nothing compared to 
the screaming audiences of The Price Is Right.
Motion Pictures
While Europe is making films like Wild Strawberries 
and He Who Must Die, we are producing I Was a Teenage 
Martian and Rock Around the Universe. What books in 
recent years have outsold Peyton Place or I, the Jury? 
This list of rubbish is endless.
Americans beware! The missionaries of Teenianity are 
everywhere, and their influence grows stronger every day. 
Even here at Tech, the first seeds of conversion have been 
sown. The most sacred of Teenian rituals has been initiated: 
the record hop. Who knows how far it will lead once the door 
is opened?

The Once Great Scot
Campus wastebaskets seemed a little fuller than usual this past week, for the Great 
Scot has again made its appearance. Alleged to be the campus humor magazine, the Great 
Scot leaves much to be desired in this area.
The one or two pieces of humor that were present (copied in part from an old Tartan 
and the Yale Record) were buried deep amid a hoard of filler, advertisements and scenes of^ 
local Pittsburgh barrooms. As even the staff of the publication admits, the Scot is 
longer a humor magazine. What the staff fails to make clear is its purpose in publishing 
at all. If a publication has no purpose of existence and fulfills no student need, then would 
not the money and efforts devoted to it be better utilized elsewhere?
One would think that a publication designed for and sold solely on the Tech campus 
would direct the majority of its content toward the interests of Tech students. Instead the 
Scot has seemed to single out as its reader the independently wealthy playboy who w&nts 
to know what set of clothes to wear while cruising around in his sports car and what plush 
night clubs to be seen in. Ask yourself, is this type of journalism to be put on a par with the 
Technical, Thistle and the Tartan? O
However, the Tartan does not want its readers to feel that it is in any way anti-Scot. 
Instead the Tartan will say much for the improved financial position of the Scot and its 
attractive format and layout. It feels that there is definite hope for the magazine in the 
future if it can find itself. The concern of the Tartan is the concern of all Techites; that this 
often controversial magazine re-examine its direction, make clear to all its aims, and finally 
to include some humor.
Merit In Coed Dining >}
Although the atmosphere of the dining room (certain to'be called Skibo) of the new 
Activities Center will be a vast improvement over the old Skibo, the humanizing influence 
of coed dining will again be missing. One is led to question the system which was formerly 
in effect and which will be in effect in the future under which dining facilities are segre
gated for the most part according to sex.
If the Tech students who use these facilities learned something more than the way to 
make it through the line fastest, than a great deal would have been accomplished. After 
all, not all facets of well-roundedness are learned in history courses. The short experience 
in coed dining which will be in effect for the remainder of this year may point the way ^ 
to something permanent for the betterment of all. J.D.S.

Ode To The Great Oral Utterance
Dedicated to lecturers who shouldnt.
Oh great and mighty Utterance 
Why do you talk so longly?
Your vocal cords sure work OK,
But the words come out so wrongly.
With flipping lips and flopping tongue, 
And teeth that click and chatter,
Your idle mind reveals its thought 
Through strange and useless chatter.
The flow of noise is great indeed! 
The volume is immense!
Your talk contains above all things 
A total lack of sense.
It seems to me beyond all doubt;
At least be it supposed
That life would be much grander yet
If youd keep your big mouth closed!
