Special Interest Houses Assigned to Five Groups
Vl E$4C H bEmb4EMME
Tartan Staffwriter
Five groups were notified last week that 
their applications for special interest houses 
were accepted. Two groups, Tech House and 
Storm, will be returning next year. The 
Swim Team, the Intercollegiate Group for 
Community Service, and the Pioneers will 
be sharing a house for the first time.
Originally, six houses were available next 
year for special interest houses. Six groups 
applied for those positions. The decision to 
demolish Tech House next year came while 
the applications were under review. Philip 
Dye, spokesman for the sixth group which
did not receive a house, feels the process was 
run unfairly because old applications were 
handed out, causing the deadline to be 
extended. Two groups applied after the 
original deadline. "Overall, the whole 
business was quite unorganized. The old 
applications shouldn't have been available," 
says Dye.
Assistant Director of Housing, Michael 
Murphy, felt the selection "went smoothly. 
[The groups'] goals are certainly ambitious 
and solid." Groups for special interest 
houses are chosen for their strength as a 
group and for their impact on a larger part of

Extra Long The Lower Depths Asks: What Keeps a Man Alive?
Vl bE!b4E L$4114-
Tartan Assistant Entertainment Editor
The Lower Depths is not entertaining. It 
doesnt keep you laughing or catch you up 
in a whirlwind plot; instead it rattles and 
dents the protective armor of our affluent 
environment with its insistent and disturbing 
questions: What does it mean to be crowded, 
hungry and hopeless? The play was written 
by Russian-born Maxim Gorky at the turn 
of the century, but its issues are timelessly 
troubling. While the Drama Departments 
production grapples with the problems of a 
young cast, an odd adaptation of the script, 
and a tiring three-hour length, it nonetheless 
succeeds in making the audience thoughtful 
and uneasy.
Even as the audience enters the theatre, the 
players lie asleep on their many berths - this 
detail has the unsettling effect of making the 
audience feel like intruders in an already toocrowded domain. The masterful set designed 
by Alla Chertok assaults the senses with its 
roughness and height. In one place it even 
juts out beyond the stage, encroaching on 
the territory of the audience. Though it 
serves as just a framework, with smaller 
props filling in for realism, the set conveys 
the underlying currents of dirt, darkness, and 
despair that the characters labor under. Beds 
are everywhere and not everyone sleeps at the 
same time - privacy is non-existent. People 
cling to any small comfort, even if it means 
being glad a sick resident has died and ceased 
her loud, racking coughs during the night.
It is the unbelievable destitution so 
excellently portrayed by the set that 
highlights the weakness of some of the 
performances. For the horror of their living 
conditions, some of the actors are simply 
too kind and too good-looking to be 
believable. Part of this problem might be 
due to the ambiguity of the adaptation by 
Margaret Thomas Kelso. While the play is 
set in Russia, the language has been
rewritten to sound somewhat American. The 
actors are faced with the double task of 
playing Russians, but doing it from an 
Americanized script. Tom Prior as Luka, the 
old man who tries to inject hope into the 
situation, seems too energetic, too naive to 
be real. For having had such a miserable life, 
Rebekah Eve Alfred as Nastya is just too 
pretty.
There are some excellent performances, 
however. David Lockhart, as the alcoholic
Actor, is believably crazed and doddering. As 
the conniving landlady Vasilisa, Laura 
Hunter-Mitchell arouses both hatred and 
curiosity. David Yezzis performance as 
Satin (pronounced sa-teen) is convincingly 
understated, and Bruce J. Faulk shines as 
Alyoshka, the accordion-playing prankster.
The Lower Depths is directed by Anthony 
McKay, whose most recent production in the 
Kresge was Four of a Kind. Stephanie 
Salzman, who recently wrote music for
Carnegie Mellons production of The 
Odyssey, contributes haunting compositions 
that tie in well with the mood of this play. 
The show is worth the time to go see 
because it will force you to think of 
questions you might otherwise ignore. The 
Lower Depths runs at 8:00 pm March 17-21 
and also at 2:00 pm on March 21. All per
formances are in Kresge Theatre. Call the 
box office at 268-2407 for more infor
mation.
