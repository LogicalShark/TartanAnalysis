Home Is Like This
I have been the daughter in my father’s house too long to 
believe that a man’s home is his castle. ..My judgement may be 
hasty. I may not have taken all my father’s blessings into ac­
count, or perhaps X have a preconceived notion of a castle that 
refuses to be compromised.. Still I feel certain that whenever 
my father has seen that pretentious statement in the school books 
his children leave about the house, or in the Sunday School pub­
lication, or in the newspaper pages advertising the August fur­
niture sales, lie has never applied it to himself. He has prob­
ably thought of it in terms of Mr. Roberts who,lives alone on the 
second floor'of the apartment next door, 10* of Mr. Shale whose 
wife vacations at the homes ol! their married children most of 
the year. But to himself—to the man who comes home in the 
evening to occupy the place at the head of the table, who is call­
ed upon to sign checks and make them effective, who receives 
all reports on a leaking roof, defective electrical attachments, 
missing keys, and automobile repairs—he has surely never said, 
“This is my own—my home—my castle.”

One Year Old
The BARD of 1940 appears in form quite different 
from that of last year’s Volume One, but in spirit quite 
the same. Its aim is to publish those things which stu­
dents have written not to please “Scottie” or “Tartan” 
editors—or English professors—but just to express their 
own ideas. Because the editors have sensed this element 
of personal expression in the contributions to the BARD, 
we have found it particularly difficult to separate the 
good from the not-so-good. We hope that we shall not 
be judged too harshly for our judgments . . . the task 
has not been easy.
We wish to express our deep gratitude to the “Tar­
tan” staff, who have made this publication possible. A 
joint committee of members of Alpha Chi Alpha and 
Pi Delta Epsilon, women’s and men’s journalistic hon- 
oraries, has edited and arranged the material, but it is 
to the students who have shown their interest by con­
tributing pieces—whether they have been used or not— 
that we owe the success of the BARD.

Campaign Poem 
Of the Third Party
Great historical events ojten give birth to literary master­
pieces. The American Revolution had Thomas Jefferson to 
write a Declaration of Independence;-The Civil War had Lin­
coln to write a Gettysburg Address, Carnegie Student Elec­
tions had Chang Dunn to write a campaign poem for the third 
party.
A. little guy named Lebovit started all this noise
Of: Independents and Big Threes which aroused all the boys.
The Big Three lost their election and were in a sort of mess; 
They got their boys in action, the Independents to suppress. 
First they stole some Tartans, using force supreme;
But they were soon retarded by our ever-smiling dean.
“Fraud,” cried the Independents, as they hurled bloody words 
“They’re stealing all our money, those guys are for the birds.” 
“Prove it,” cried the Big Three, as they stuck their noses high, 
“You haven’t got a thing on us,” cried Beta Theta Pi,
And so cried Kappa Sigma, and so cried Pi K A,
And the Independents proved it, much to Big Three dismay. 
Now the Independents had a foothold, but soon there were many 
groans
For a folly they committed—they put up three unknowns. 
Instead of becoming stronger they weakened slow but sure 
And now tlieir ticket smelled just like a rusty sewer.
The names of Friedlander. and Schmidt then suddenly arose 
To form a third party—to add to the political woes.
Again the Independents cried, for again they were quite sad, 
But now their cries were unheeded; no sympathy to be had. 
“They’re ineligible! They’re ineligible!” And loudly did they 
wail
And again from the Independents came they very ghastly smell. 
You know the story now, Big Three and Independents are the 
goats.
Schmidt and Friedlander, Friedlander and Schmidt—to them 
you owe your votes.
