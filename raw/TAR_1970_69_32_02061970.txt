Page 2—THE TARTAN—Friday, February 6, 1970 •
EDITORIAL
How many more incidents will it take before we wake 
up and improve and increase the security forces on campus. 
Time and time again we yell, “We need more security offi­
cers!” Over and over we scream that the lighting on campus 
is inadequate.
Yet incident upon incident threatens the safety of the 
campus. The increasing crime rate in the cities cannot ignore 
our ivory tower. Last year alone 662 thefts were reported on 
campus. Assaults have increased 136 percent over last year. 
Prowlers were up 200 percent. An average of four cars are 
stolen on campus each year. The facts speak for themselves; 
the crime rate on campus has significantly increased.
It is frightening to note that there are only twenty-one 
assignable C-MU security men to cover the campus twenty- 
four hours per day, seven days per week. As WRCT said in 
an October editorial, “Working only one shift per day with 
two days off per week, there remains a dangerously small 
number of security patrolman on duty at any one time.” Only 
four officers were on duty covering the entire campus Tuesday 
night at the time of the Hammerschlag incident. Certainly 
there is a shortage of security patrolmen.
Several steps must be taken immediately to curb the pre­
sent crime wave. We recommend that the security force be 
increased by fifty per cent. The present force cannot possibly 
answer all the calls which it receives. Last year alone the 
Security Department responded to 15,287 calls. This year 
there are more.
Although we must compliment the officer who respond­
ed to the call Tuesday night for notifying city police within 
seconds, there is no reason why a secretary could not as ef­
fectively handled the call. This would mean that one more 
officer would be available for active duty.
We further recommend that all calls to the Security De­
partment be answered immediately. Checking out the call 
only results in a dangerous time delay. The few false alarms 
would thoroughly justify the possibilities of getting to the 
scene of a crime immediately.
In addition, the lighting on campus must be increased. 
The wattage currently being used is insufficient. The walk 
between the library and Morewood, for example, leaves one 
prey to campus prowlers. In a recent walk across the campus 
at night we did not observe one security officer who was 
available to respond immediately to a call for help. Extra 
uniformed foot patrolmen would act as a deterrent to crimes 
as well.
Individuals must also be more aware and concerned with 
the problem. Increasing the size of the force will not by it­
self alleviate the threats. Be alert. If you see someone solicit­
ing magazines or anything else in your dormitory, check to 
see that he has identification and a permission slip from the 
Division of Student Affairs. Such room to room selling offers 
a potential burglar the perfect opportunity to case the joint.
Lock your door at all times. Security reports indicate 
that burglarized rooms have been unlocked. Even when you go 
down the hall to the shower or just to visit a friend, lock 
your door. When you go to sleep, lock your door. Rooms can 
be entered when you are asleep.
If you are missing something, report the theft immediately 
to Security. Sometimes a pattern can be detected from a 
series of reported thefts. If Security can detect a trend it 
will be easier for them to apprehend the suspect and thereby 
prevent further thefts.
Be careful. Report any suspicious persons to Security. 
Don’t let it go by with an “I didn’t think it was important.” 
You are only offering an invitation to the offender to con­
tinue his crime.
The problem is urgent. It is critical. It concerns us all. 
The administration must increase the Security Department’s 
budget. Students must be more responsible.
WAKE UP! THE TIME IS NOW.
