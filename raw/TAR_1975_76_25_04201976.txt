editor’s note
The Ivory Tower
CMU has a problem with its public image. The name Car­
negie -Mellon just doesn’t generate the same respect as 
Harvard or MIT. Especially poor is the thistle symbol. What 
kind of image does a thistle provoke—thorns. A thistle with" 
its thorns just isn't appropriate. Some students do manage to 
get jabbed or stuck but that’s usually their own fault. Perhaps 
a much more descriptive symbol would be a ravine , • a val­
ley or even an abyss.
Physically, much of the univeristy is surrounded by ra­
vines. Socially and academically abysses crisscross the cam­
pus, neatly dividing the university into groups intellectually 
isolated from each other and the world.
With this isolation it is easy not to see how well our cam­
pus community acts as a model for the problems of the out­
side world. This last year the university has been facing an 
over population problem. As with the world’s population, it 
doesn’t look like the situation will improve soon. Any over- 
crowdinq, however, can be used as a way to gain information 
about the overpopulation which is facing the world.
But our isolation here at CMU tends to make us forget 
about population problems. That went out of vogue when 
the energy crisis came in. People just don’t see that the ener­
gy shortage is a result of there being too many people. A 
mathematical model developed 15 years ago presents a 
striking insight into the situation. This equation, first pub­
lished in 1960, has been shown to predict world population 
better than any other model available. What is amazing about 
this model is that is predicts that by the year 2026 the 
world’s population will be infinite. That’s only 50years 
away. Students here now will be about 70 then, so many of 
us can expect to live to witness this acheivement.
Recently the Nobel Laureate Linus Pauling predicted that 
in 25-50 years "there will occyr the greatest catastrophe in 
the history of the world. ”
5o why isn’t the university taking advantage of this by 
providing curricula relevant to these problems of the future, 
something no university anywhere now provides?
We can count on increasing shortages tor years to come 
and we can anticipate technology becoming more important 
as we attempt to meet these shortages.
Already recent technological developments demonstrate 
this. The microprocessor is enabling electric power companies 
to develop systems whereby they can control the supply of 
electricity by actually shutting off high load appliances in 
your home when demand on generating equipment becomes 
too great.
Fortunately power companies have recognized the prob­
lem of trying to prevent this method of power conservation 
from becoming an intrusion into a person’s civil liberties ;the 
potential for abuse however, is great. Adding to the danger 
is the fact that engineers just aren’t trained to handle these 
questions of technology threatening civil liberties, which we 
can expect to see more of in the future.
But engineers will probably receive the blame if tech­
nology is abused. The situation could become very similiar 
to what Aleksandr Solzhenitzyn has described in Russia 
after the revolution. All engineers were mistrusted and sus­
pected as saboteurs because only they had the technical 
knowledge to perform the sabotage which was thought to be 
the reason for slow technological advancement.
Technologists must work to avoid the same mistrust and 
suspicion which may result because they control things which 
most people don‘t understand.
Our own university illustrates how easily this can happen. 
Most students associate almost exclusively with people 
from their department or college. Ignorance through lack of 
contact tends to make people feel that anyone in another 
part of the universty is strange and somehow can’t be trusted.
The administration contributes greatly to this isolationism. 
When they extoll the fine points, we always hear great praise 
for the same people: our business school is great, our drama 
department is great, our computer science department is 
great. Is the rest of the university so bad that the administra­
tion can’t think of good things to say about them, It’s no 
wonder there’s mistrust among departments.
Even if the university won’t adopt the ravine as a symbol, 
hopefully in the future CMU will at least recognize the abys­
ses which tend, through isolation, to distort our perspective.
Charles Mitch, Editor
